Affect —Noun: A feeling or emotion
Verb: To act on; produce an effect or change in

ASL — American Sign Language

ATA — American Translators Association

Attitude/Skill-Centered Approach — An approach to developing cultural competency in which an individual enhances their communication skills and focuses on the cultural values and beliefs of individuals (including their own) (See also Fact-Centered Approach.)

BATHE —Noun: A model used to promote culturally competent communication between a provider and a patient that brings out the patient's psychological and social contexts by asking simple questions about background, affect (feeling state), trouble, handling, and empathy. BATHE stands for:
   B — Background
   A — Affect
   T — Trouble
   H — Handling
   E — Empathy

Brown-Bag Medication Review — A method for identifying patients with limited health literacy in which patients bring all their medications (prescription and over-the-counter medications, nutritional, and herbal supplements, etc.) to their appointments, and are asked by their provider to name each medication and explain its purpose and how it is taken.

Capstone Activities —The activities provided in Module 5 that integrate the concepts and techniques learned in the health literacy, cultural competency, and limited English proficiency modules.

Civic Literacy — The skills and abilities that enable citizens to become aware of public issues, participate in critical dialogues about them, and become involved in decision-making processes. (See also Fundamental Literacy, Scientific Literacy, and Cultural Literacy.)

Consecutive Interpreting — The mode of interpreting where the speaker and the interpreter take turns speaking (the interpreter waits until the speaker has finished the utterance before rendering it in the other language) (See also Simultaneous Interpreting, Summary/Paraphrasing Interpreting, and Sight Translation.)

Continuum of Care — The entire spectrum of specialized health, rehabilitative, and residential services available to the frail and chronically ill. The services focus on the social, residential, rehabilitative and supportive needs of individuals as well as needs that are essentially medical in nature.

CRASH — A model used to promote culturally competent communication between a provider and a patient that represents the values that help create a framework for providing culturally competent health care. CRASH stands for:
   C — Consider culture
   R — Respect
   A — Assess and affirm
   S — Sensitivity and self-awareness
   H — Humility

Cultural Competency — A set of attitudes, skills, behaviors, and policies that enable organizations and staff to work effectively in cross-cultural situations. It reflects the ability to acquire and use knowledge of the health-related beliefs, attitudes, practices, and communication patterns of clients and their families to improve services, strengthen programs, increase community participation, and close the gaps in health status among diverse population groups.

Cultural Competency Framework — A framework used to assess an individual's cultural competency development that focuses on four areas:
   • Intercultural concepts
   • Intercultural knowledge
   • Intercultural skills
   • Intercultural attitudes

Cultural Literacy — The abilities needed to recognize, understand, and use the collective beliefs, customs, worldview, and social identity of diverse individuals to interpret and act on information. (See also Fundamental Literacy, Scientific Literacy, and Civic Literacy.)

Culture — An integrated pattern of human behavior that includes thoughts, communications, languages, practices, beliefs, values, customs, courtesies, rituals, manners of interacting, roles, relationships, and expected behaviors of a racial, ethnic, religious, or social group. Culture involves the ability to transmit the above to succeeding generations and is dynamic in nature.

Culture Clarifier — The interpreter role in which the interpreter provides a necessary cultural framework for understanding the message being interpreted and bridges the communication gap between the parties who are not a part of a common culture. (See also Message Converter, Message Clarifier, and Patient Advocate.)

DOJ — Department of Justice

Empathy — Identification with and understanding of another's situation, feelings, and motives

ESFT — A model used to promote culturally competent communication between a provider and a patient that guides providers in understanding a patient’s explanatory model (a patient’s conception of his or her illness), social and environmental factors, fears and concerns, and in contracting for therapeutic approaches. ESFT stands for:
   E — Explanatory
   S — Social
   F — Fears
   T — Therapeutic

ETHNIC — A model used to promote culturally competent communication between a provider and a patient that includes questions to bring out information about a patient’s explanation of illness, treatment, and healers, along with negotiation, intervention, and collaboration about treatment. ETHNIC stands for:
   E — Explanation
   T — Treatment
   H — Healers
   N — Negotiation
   I — Intervention
   C — Collaboration

Ethnosensitivity — The process of accepting, respecting, and valuing cultural differences diversity among individuals

Executive Order 13166 — Federal funding agencies to create a plan to provide meaningful access to limited English proficient (LEP) individuals to their Federally conducted programs. In addition, it requires federal funding agencies to provide guidance to their recipients on how to take reasonable steps to provide meaningful access to their programs to LEP individuals in compliance with the nondiscrimination provisions of Title VI and its implementing regulations.

Fact-Centered Approach — An approach to developing cultural competency in which an individual gains cultural information about specific ethnic groups (See also Attitude/Skill-Centered Approach.)

First-Person Speech — When an interpreter interprets the message as if he or she were speaking in the patient’s or the provider’s voice. For example:
   Provider: When did it start hurting?
   Interpreter: When did it start hurting?
(See also Third-Person Speech.)

Fundamental Literacy — The ability to read, write, speak, and work with numbers. (See also Scientific Literacy, Civic Literacy, and Cultural Literacy.)

Back to top

 

Health Communication — The study and use of communication strategies to inform and influence individual and community decisions that enhance health. Health communication links the fields of communication and health, and is increasingly recognized as a necessary element of efforts to improve personal and public health.

Health Literacy — Health literacy is the degree to which individuals can obtain, process, and understand basic health information and services needed to make appropriate health decisions.

HHS — Department of Health and Human Services

HHS Revised LEP Guidance (Guidance to Federal Financial Assistance Recipients Regarding Title VI Prohibition Against National Origin Discrimination Affecting Limited English Proficient Persons) — HHS's LEP guidance as required by Executive Order 13166. This guidance is mandatory for all HHS grantees.

HRSA — Health Resources and Services Administration

Informed Consent — The process of obtaining a patient's permission for a procedure after the patient and doctor have discussed the risks, benefits, and alternatives of the procedure and the patient understands them.

Interpretation — The process of understanding and analyzing a spoken or signed message and re-expressing that message faithfully, accurately, and objectively in another language, taking the cultural and social contexts into account

"I Speak" Cards and Posters — Tools for identifying a patient’s language that feature the same information printed in English and several other languages, and provide a way for the patient to indicate his or her language to the provider

Back to top

 

Kleinman’s Nine Questions — A tool, developed by Dr. Arthur Kleinman, consisting of nine questions used to bring out a patient's health beliefs. The questions are:
   • What do you call your problem? What name does it have?
   • What do you think caused your problem?
   • Why do you think it started when it did?
   • What does your sickness do to you? How does it work?
   • How severe is it? Will it have a short or long course?
   • What do you fear most about your disorder?
   • What are the chief problems that your sickness has caused for you?
   • What kind of treatment do you think you should receive?
   • What are the most important results you hope to receive from the treatment?

LEARN — A model used to promote culturally competent communication between a provider and patient that provides a framework of listening, explaining, acknowledging, recommending, and negotiating. LEARN stands for:
   L — Listen
   E — Explain
   A — Acknowledge
   R — Recommend
   N — Negotiate

Limited English Proficiency (LEP) — A limited ability to read, write, speak or understand English. A limited English proficient individual is one who does not speak English as primary language and the individual may be eligible to receive language assistance with respect to a particular type of service, benefit, or encounter.

Message Clarifier — The interpreter role where the interpreter explains or makes word pictures, word analogies, or gestures of terms that can be easily understood by the listener, and then checks for the listener’s understanding. (See also Message Converter, Culture Clarifier, and Patient Advocate.)

Message Converter — The interpreter role where the interpreter conveys meaning in one language literally from what has been said in another language, with no additions, omissions, or editing. (See also Message Clarifier, Culture Clarifier, and Patient Advocate.)

Mode — With regards to interpreting, mode is the style or manner of interpretation

Newest Vital Sign Assessment — A screening tool for identifying patients at risk for limited health literacy, that involves the patient interpreting information provided on a nutrition label from an ice cream container.

OMH — Office of Minority Health

Back to top

 

Patient-Centered Perspective — A patient-centered perspective reflects the realities of people’s everyday lives and their current practices, attitudes, and beliefs, and lifestyles. This means considering the characteristics of your patients—including gender, age, education and income level, ethnicity, sexual orientation, cultural beliefs and values, primary language(s), and physical and mental functioning.

Pre-Interview Session — The first component of the triadic interview process where a brief meeting is held between the interpreter and the provider before the interpreted encounter occurs, in order to establish ground rules and clarify the purpose of the patient-provider interaction. (See also Triadic Interview and Post-Interview Debriefing.)

Post-Interview Debriefing — The third and final component of the triadic interview process used to determine next steps, clarify the interpreter’s view of the meeting, validate the interpreter’s role, and so forth. The Post-Interview Session is optional, to be used only when necessary. (See also Pre-Interview Session and Triadic Interview.)

Rapid Estimate of Adult Literacy in Medicine (REALM) — A word recognition test used to assess a patient's general reading skills and identify a patient's health literacy level

RID — Registry of Interpreters for the Deaf

Back to top

 

Scientific Literacy — The skills and abilities needed to understand and use science and technology, including some awareness of the process of science. (See also Fundamental Literacy, Civic Literacy, and Cultural Literacy.)

Seven Stages of a Developmental Model of Ethnosensitivity for Family Practice Training — A progressive, seven-stage model of cultural competency development, with Stage 1 being the lowest level of cultural competency development and Stage 7 being the highest:
   Stage 1: Fear
   Stage 2: Denial
   Stage 3: Superiority
   Stage 4: Minimization
   Stage 5: Relativism
   Stage 6: Empathy
   Stage 7: Integration

Sight Translation — The mode of interpreting where the interpreter reads a written document and orally interprets it in the targeted language(s) (See also Consecutive Interpreting, Simultaneous Interpreting, and Summary/Paraphrasing Interpreting.)

Simultaneous Interpreting — The mode of interpreting where the interpreter translates with a slight lag time after the speaker, and interprets at the same time as the speaker is talking (See also Consecutive Interpreting, Summary/Paraphrasing Interpreting, and Sight Translation.)

Stereotype — A generalization, usually exaggerated or oversimplified and often offensive, that is used to describe or distinguish a group

Summary/Paraphrasing Interpreting — The mode of interpreting where one person speaks for some time and the interpreter summarizes the main points at the end (See also Consecutive Interpreting, Simultaneous Interpreting, and Sight Translation.)

Back to top

 

Teach Back/Show Me Method — A method used by providers to ensure their patients understand their message. The Teach Back method starts with the health care provider demonstrating or explaining the new concept, then assessing patient recall and understanding by asking the patient to describe (Teach Back) or demonstrate (Show Me) the concept in his or her own words or actions.

Test of Functional Health Literacy in Adults (TOFHLA) — An assessment tool used to identify a patient's level of health literacy that involves the patient answering questions about medical information or instructions.

Third-Person Speech — When an interpreter interprets the message as if the he/she were speaking about the patient or provider. For example:
   Provider: When did it start hurting?
   Interpreter: The doctor wants to know when it started hurting.
(See also First-Person Speech.)

Title VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 — "No person in the United States shall, on ground of race, color, or national origin, be excluded from participation in, be denied the benefits of, or be subjected to discrimination under any program or activity receiving Federal financial assistance.”

Translation — The replacement of a text written in one language (source language) by an equivalent text written in another language (target language)

Transparency — With regards to interpreting, transparency is when everything said by any party, including the interpreter, is interpreted in a language that others can understand

Triadic Interview — The second component of the triadic interview process where the actual interpreted interaction occurs (See also Pre-Interview Session and Post-Interview Debriefing.)

Triadic Interview Process — The most effective way to conduct an interpreted interaction. The triadic interview process consists of the following components:
   • A pre-interview session
   • The triadic interview
   • A post-interview debriefing (when necessary)

Back to top

 

Unified Approach to Health Communication — The approach to health communication that involves being aware of the unique needs of patients and understanding the influences of patients’ health literacy levels, unique cultural backgrounds, and English language proficiency levels have on their ability to make informed health care decisions, and to participate fully in recommendations to improve their health outcomes

Universal Symbols — Symbols that are universally understood, such as an airplane, as opposed to symbols that are subject to the viewer’s interpretation, such as a cross or money
